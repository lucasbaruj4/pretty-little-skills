# Make It Fun

Anxiety feeds on importance. The fix isn't to lower the actual stakes of the task — it's to stop treating it like a matter of life and death. Do the interview *for fun*. Submit the documents *for fun*. The task stays real; only the weight comes off. Every task qualifies, however heavy it feels — the heavier ones need the reframe most, so play it for any of them.

## How to build one

1. **Read the task, spot the weight.** What's making it feel heavy — an evaluator judging them, an irreversible deadline, an unknown outcome? Name it to yourself, don't repeat it back solemnly.
2. **Pick a game that fits its shape.** Match the genre to the task, don't force one template:
   - Being evaluated (interview, audition, review) → a performance, a boss fight, a bit.
   - Paperwork/bureaucracy (visa docs, forms, applications) → a scavenger hunt, a puzzle, collecting items.
   - A first/unfamiliar situation (new job, new place) → an expedition, exploring a new map.
   - A hard conversation → a side quest, a negotiation minigame.
   If none fit, invent one rather than stretching a bad match.
3. **Write it short and playful.** A few punchy lines, not an essay: a win condition, a score, a made-up rule or two, maybe a title. Wit over whimsy — it should make them smirk, not read like a children's book.
4. **Hand them a throwaway line.** One quirky thing they can actually say out loud mid-task to prove to themselves they're not taking it seriously — sarcastic, deadpan, self-aware, or plain absurd. It has to fit their real situation and be sayable. E.g. opening a call with "hi, it's the annoying student who phones you every week."
5. **Give them a ridiculous fixation, phrased as a question.** Something arbitrary and dumb to quietly wonder about *during* the task, so their attention has somewhere stupid to go instead of the stakes. Always a question *to them*, never an instruction: "also — what haircut would actually suit your interviewer?" or "have you ever wondered what your boss's favourite colour is?" The more pointless, the better it works.
6. **Keep the real action legible underneath.** The game wrapper sits on top of the actual steps, not instead of them — someone should be able to tell what they're really about to go do.
7. **Close with the one real next step**, stated plainly, no game language, so the actual to-do doesn't get lost in the bit.
8. **Never act on their behalf.** Only write the reframe. Don't send, submit, email, or schedule anything — hand it back for them to read first.
